# Reps in Reserve (RIR)

## Definition

RIR = how many more reps you COULD have completed with perfect form before reaching technical failure.

Also known as the reverse of RPE:  
**RIR = 10 - RPE**

## RIR Reference

| RIR | Description | RPE Equivalent |
|-----|-------------|----------------|
| 0 RIR | Technical failure — cannot complete another rep | RPE 10 |
| 1 RIR | Very hard — could do 1 more rep | RPE 9 |
| 2 RIR | Hard — could do 2 more reps | RPE 8 |
| 3 RIR | Moderate — could do 3 more reps | RPE 7 |
| 4 RIR | Light — could do 4+ more reps | RPE 6 |

## RIR Targets by Exercise Type

| Exercise Type | RIR Target | Why |
|--------------|------------|-----|
| Compound (bench, row, OHP, squat) | 2-3 RIR | Safety, CNS fatigue management |
| Secondary compound | 1-2 RIR | More stimulus, still controlled |
| Machine compound | 1-2 RIR | Safer than free weights for proximity |
| Isolation (curls, raises, extensions) | 0-2 RIR | Can push closer to failure safely |
| Abs / calves | 0-1 RIR | High fatigue resistance |

## When to Use RIR

- Primary training method (RPE-based training)
- When auto-regulating session intensity
- When recovering from illness/injury (increase RIR)
- On deload weeks (increase RIR to 3-4)

## Limitations

- Requires honest self-assessment (beginners overestimate RIR)
- Inconsistent day-to-day fatigue affects perception
- Not useful for very high rep sets (20+) — metabolically challenging
- Better to UNDERestimate RIR (leave more in tank) than overestimate
