# DOMS (Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness)

## What Is DOMS?

Muscle soreness that appears 12-24 hours after training, peaks at 24-72 hours, and gradually subsides over 3-7 days.

**Not an indicator of muscle growth.** Soreness ≠ hypertrophy.

## Causes

- **Eccentric contractions** — the primary driver of DOMS
- **Novel movements** — new exercises or training styles
- **High volume** — returning after a layoff or deload
- **Muscle damage** — micro-tears in muscle fibres (part of adaptation)

## What DOMS Is NOT

- A sign of a "good workout" (chronic soreness = poor recovery)
- Required for muscle growth (you can grow without ever being sore)
- An excuse to skip training (light movement helps)

## Managing DOMS

| Method | Effectiveness | Notes |
|--------|--------------|-------|
| Light movement | Good | Walking, light cycling — don't train the sore muscle hard |
| Compression garments | Mild | May reduce perception of soreness |
| Massage | Moderate | Temporarily reduces soreness |
| Foam rolling | Mild | Short-term relief |
| Ice bath | Mixed | May reduce soreness but blunts adaptation |
| NSAIDs (ibuprofen) | Effective | NOT recommended — blunts hypertrophy adaptation |
| Stretching | Minimal | No evidence it prevents or treats DOMS |

## When to Train with DOMS

- **Upper body DOMS:** Can train lower body (and vice versa)
- **Mild DOMS:** Light training of the affected area is fine
- **Moderate DOMS:** Reduce volume by 30-50%, keep intensity
- **Severe DOMS:** Consider an extra rest day or deload

## Key Points

- DOMS decreases as you become accustomed to a training stimulus
- First week back from deload or layoff = highest DOMS
- DOMS is a side effect, not a goal
- Train through mild DOMS; rest through severe DOMS
- If DOMS lasts >5 days consistently, check sleep and nutrition
